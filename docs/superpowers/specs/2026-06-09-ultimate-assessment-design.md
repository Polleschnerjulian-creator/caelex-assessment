# Ultimate Operator Assessment — Design (Konzept zur Abnahme)

> Erstellt 2026-06-09 aus 3 Research-Lanes (interne Sezierung, Regulatorik-Web-Recherche, Assessment-Design-Recherche) + Synthese + adversarialem Anwalts/Growth-Review. Status: ENTWURF — wartet auf Founder-Abnahme. Kein Code vor Abnahme.

## 1. Urteil über den Ist-Zustand

KEEP THE ENGINES, REBUILD THE ASSESSMENT AROUND THEM. The four wizards are honest-looking facades over genuinely good machinery. What is worth keeping unchanged: the server-only engine architecture with redaction (src/lib/engine.server.ts + caelex-eu-space-act-engine.json with 119 articles/excludes/TCO handling; classifyNIS2Entity in nis2-engine.server.ts with its defensible Art 2/3/26 rule ordering; the 10-jurisdiction dataset in national-space-laws.ts; the unified merger's article-dedupe and most-restrictive logic in unified-engine-merger.server.ts), the API hygiene (Zod, rate limits, audit chain), and the wizard UI primitives (QuestionStep/OptionCard/ProgressBar). What must be REBUILT, not patched: (1) the question layer — across all four modules most questions are cosmetic (EU wizard: only 3 of 9 answers change the verdict while subtitles promise constellation-size-dependent requirements that the engine never computes; NIS2 standalone: 7 questions, 1 decisive input, its own engine's best path (Art 26) unreachable; unified: ~59 questions, roughly a third consumed, with helpText promising a TPL-vs-mass calculation that does not exist); (2) the honesty layer — false-green paths ('Unknown' stored as value:false on isEssentialServiceProvider; insufficient NIS2 data rendered as 'does not apply'; redaction stripping the engine's own 'not empirically validated' disclaimer; three fabricated 'structural overlaps' when none are found; hard-stops enforced only client-side so an empty API payload yields a confident verdict); (3) the funnel — lead capture writes to the visitor's own localStorage with a false privacy claim and a pre-checked newsletter box that subscribes nobody; (4) the dual-dataset problem — dashboard import recomputes applicability from a second, drift-prone dataset (src/data/articles.ts) instead of the engine result the user just saw. Verdict: EXTEND the engines (NIS2 gateway completeness, real regime eligibility, constellation-tier consumption, flux flags, version stamps), REBUILD the question graph, scoring pipeline, and result page as ONE branching assessment with two tiers, and RETIRE the four standalone wizards as separate logic paths (keep their URLs as SEO entry points that deep-link into the new spine with the relevant section preselected). Independently of the rebuild, six dishonesty items should be fixed immediately on the current product because they are live trust/GDPR liabilities: the Unknown→false mapping, the localStorage 'lead capture' + false privacy copy + pre-checked consent, the stripped confidence disclaimers, the fabricated overlaps, the decorative bot check (optional startedAt), and the client-only hard-stops.

## 2. Befunde der internen Sezierung (Lane A)

### Schwächen

- COSMETIC QUESTIONS / CORE OVERCLAIM (EU Space Act): of the 9 questions, only activityType, establishment, and entitySize actually change the verdict; isDefenseOnly/hasPostLaunchAssets/establishment are hard-stop gates; constellation (Q6/Q7), orbit (Q8) and offersEUServices (Q9) feed ONLY display labels. filterArticlesByOperator (engine.server.ts:167) filters articles solely by operator abbreviation + TCO — a 1-satellite operator and a 1,000-satellite mega-constellation get IDENTICAL articles, modules, checklist and cost. Yet Q6's subtitle promises 'Constellations have additional requirements based on size' and Q7's options promise 'Enhanced debris + pollution mitigation mandatory'. The wizard asks questions whose answers it visibly pretends matter.
- REGIME DETERMINATION IS ONE LINE: light regime = entitySize ∈ {small, research} (engine.server.ts:543). No balance-sheet test, no group/linked-enterprise aggregation (EU SME definition aggregates parents — a 30-person Airbus subsidiary self-declares 'small' and is told Light Regime), no eligibility conditions from Art. 10, no check that a small entity running a mega-constellation should still qualify. A lawyer would call the regime verdict a heuristic presented as a determination.
- HARD-STOPS ENFORCED ONLY CLIENT-SIDE: hasPostLaunchAssets is never read by calculateCompliance — the 'grandfathered, out of scope' verdict for pre-2030 assets exists only in questions.ts outOfScopeValue. A direct API call with hasPostLaunchAssets=false returns a full compliance result. Worse, ALL fields in EUSpaceActAnswersSchema are nullable/optional, so an empty answers object yields a confident 'General (activity-agnostic)' standard-regime result fabricated from zero information, with no confidence indicator.
- NIS2 WIZARD CANNOT REACH ITS OWN ENGINE'S BEST LOGIC: the standalone wizard hard-stops at Q2 for non-EU entities, so the Art. 26 path (non-EU + EU services → important + representative obligation) is unreachable; designatedByMemberState, offersServicesInEU, and providesDigitalInfrastructure are never asked standalone and stay null. Classification therefore collapses to entitySize alone for EU space entities — Q4 (memberStateCount) only changes a supervisory-authority note, and Q5-Q7 (ground infra, ISO 27001, CSIRT) have no effect on classification at all. 7 questions, 1 decisive input.
- UNIFIED COLLECTS ~59 QUESTIONS BUT CONSUMES ROUGHLY A THIRD: launchTimeline, missionDuration, governmentContracts, partOfSupplyChain, requiresEnglishProcess, prefersFastProcessing, usesRadioFrequencies, frequencyBands, insuranceCoverage, insuranceAmount, hasThirdPartyLiability, complianceBudget, hasLegalCounsel, isStartup, isInternationalOrg/internationalOrgType, euControlledEntity (for Space Act scope) and dataProviderTypes are never referenced by the mappers (unified-assessment-mappers.server.ts), merger, or route. helpText actively overpromises analysis that does not exist: insuranceCoverage claims amounts are 'compared against calculated minimum TPL requirements based on your orbit, spacecraft mass, and mission type per Art. 48' — there is no mass question and no TPL calculation anywhere; the Art. 2(2) international-organisation exemption is asked about but never applied; the Art. 2(1)(c) EU-control scope rule is asked but ignored by deriveEstablishment.
- FALSE-GREEN PATHS EXIST: (a) isEssentialServiceProvider's 'Unknown' option has value:false — uncertainty is recorded as a definitive No on a scope-relevant question (unified-assessment-questions.ts:977-983); (b) NIS2 classification with missing entitySize returns classification 'out_of_scope', which the unified merger renders as nis2.applies=false — insufficient data becomes 'NIS2 doesn't apply'; (c) employeeRange/turnoverRange are mapped to midpoints but classifyNIS2Entity only ever reads entitySize, so a user who picks 'small' but '€50-250M turnover' is still out-of-scope; (d) hasInsurance null is deliberately not a gap.
- HONESTY STRIPPED BY REDACTION: redactNIS2ResultForClient (nis2-engine.server.ts:455) removes confidenceLevel and estimationSource from euSpaceActOverlap — the engine's own 'Internal estimate — not empirically validated' disclaimer never reaches the user, who sees 'X weeks potential savings' as if validated. Similarly, buildCrossFrameworkOverlap FABRICATES three hardcoded 'structural overlaps' when the real cross-reference lookup finds none (unified-engine-merger.server.ts:264-283) instead of honestly reporting none found.
- HAND-WAVY NUMBERS PRESENTED AS FACTS: estimatedAuthorizationCost is hardcoded prose ('~€100,000 per satellite platform', '€150,000-300,000 per launch system') with no source or uncertainty band, displayed prominently in ComplianceProfile; estimatedMonths in unified is a crude additive (light=6, standard=12, +NIS2 6/9, +3 if any jurisdiction, cap 24); risk thresholds (12/8/4) and gap weights are unexplained constants; '1 Jan 2030 — Application date, prepare now' is rendered as a fixed deadline for a regulation that is still a legislative proposal, with no proposal-version stamp on stored results.
- FAVORABILITY SCORE IS PSEUDO-QUANTIFIED ARBITRAGE ADVICE: shared.server.ts calculateFavorabilityScore awards +15 for fast timelines, +10 for indemnification, +8 for capped liability etc. on an arbitrary scale, then the engine recommends 'X scores highest (NN/100) — consider it as your primary jurisdiction'. Value judgments are baked in: 'insurance not mandatory' scores 5 vs 3 (less protection = more favorable), 'application fee not specified' scores best (missing data rewarded), and the application_fee criterion contains dead logic — `fee.includes("€") ? 3 : 3` (space-law-engine.server.ts:582-587), so every fee-charging jurisdiction scores an identical 3. Regulatory maturity hardcodes the year 2026. Space-law Q5 (orbit) is unused by the generic engine (only a UK-profile boolean), licensingStatus and constellationSize each drive a single canned recommendation string — more cosmetic questions.
- LEAD CAPTURE IS FAKE AND ITS PRIVACY COPY IS FALSE: EmailGate leads are pushed into the VISITOR'S OWN localStorage ('caelex-assessment-leads', ResultsDashboard.tsx:42-53) and never sent to any server or CRM — the business loses every lead. Meanwhile the modal claims 'Your data is not stored or shared' (it is stored, locally) and the newsletter checkbox is PRE-CHECKED (subscribe default true) while no subscription is performed — a GDPR-shaped promise about a feature that doesn't exist.
- DASHBOARD IMPORT DISCARDS THE ASSESSMENT'S NUANCE AND USES A SECOND, DIVERGEABLE DATASET: /api/tracker/import-assessment ignores the entire ComplianceResult it receives except operatorType, then recomputes applicability from src/data/articles.ts (~75 entries, appliesTo only — no excludes, no TCO handling, no light regime, no defense exemption), while the assessment itself was computed from caelex-eu-space-act-engine.json (119 articles with excludes/TCO). Two parallel regulatory datasets that can silently drift means the dashboard tracker may not match the result the user just saw. The unified save route does the same and silently defaults unmapped activity labels to 'SCO'.
- BOT 'DETECTION' IS DECORATIVE: every endpoint guards with `if (startedAt && typeof startedAt === 'number')` while startedAt is optional in all schemas — a bot simply omits the field and the 3-second check never runs.
- UX/FUNNEL INCONSISTENCY AND GAPS: three different gating models (EU Space Act results free + email-for-PDF; NIS2/space-law results free; unified results require full account creation with a 12-char password) for what is marketed as one assessment suite; no review-your-answers step before submission in any wizard; no answer summary in results (users cannot verify what they answered); no per-answer edit; no 'unsure' option anywhere except the one that maps to false; standalone wizards have no save/resume; the unified wizard's ~59 questions take a long time for a verdict largely determined by ~6 of them. Sophisticated operators/lawyers will also notice contradictions are not detected (e.g., establishment='third_country_eu_services' + offersEUServices=No is accepted silently).
- UK DELEGATION SWALLOWS FAILURES SILENTLY: calculateUKJurisdictionResult catches any UK-engine error and falls back to the generic calculation with no logging or user-visible flag, so a UK result can quietly degrade in fidelity while still displaying engine-grade confidence (favorability bonus '+5 low regulatory risk' included).

### Fehlende Dimensionen

- Spacecraft mass / physical characteristics — drives debris-mitigation class, casualty-risk and insurance minimums in nearly every regime; never asked in any module (and the unified insurance helpText falsely implies mass is used)
- Maneuverability / propulsion capability — determines collision-avoidance obligations and conjunction-response duties; never asked
- Orbital altitude within LEO and disposal strategy (controlled vs uncontrolled reentry, deorbit timeline) — the 25-year/5-year deorbit question cannot be assessed from 'LEO/MEO/GEO/beyond'
- Launch state / state of registry nexus — UN Liability & Registration Convention attribution and which state's law actually attaches; no question asks where or with whom the operator launches
- Launch provider / launch site country — affects national licensing applicability and ITAR exposure; absent everywhere
- Ground segment locations (countries of ground stations / TT&C / mission control) — central to NIS2 jurisdiction and national-law applicability; only inferred crudely from service types in the unified mapper, never asked directly
- ITU filing / spectrum coordination status — frequency BANDS are asked (unified only) but filing status, coordination stage and national frequency license are not; the spectrum engine is never invoked by any assessment
- Existing national space licenses (standalone EU Space Act wizard) — an operator already licensed in France answers nothing about it and receives the same greenfield checklist
- Mission/licensing timeline in the standalone EU wizard — beyond the binary 'launched after 2030?' there is no stage question, so pre-revenue concept-stage and operational constellations get identical urgency framing
- Corporate group structure / linked-enterprise aggregation — EU SME size (and thus Light Regime + NIS2 size cap) must aggregate parent/linked companies; only self-declared headcount/turnover is asked
- Balance-sheet total — both the SME definition and NIS2 size caps use turnover OR balance sheet; only turnover is captured
- Sole-provider / societal-criticality status (NIS2 Art. 2(2)(b)-(e)) — the size-cap exceptions beyond formal designation are never asked, despite being exactly the trap small space operators fall into
- Export-control exposure (ITAR/EAR/EU dual-use items) — only asked as 'do you hold ITAR/EAR certification' in unified P8; no module asks whether US-origin components or controlled technology are involved, despite a dedicated dashboard module existing
- EO data sensitivity / resolution thresholds — the trigger for Germany's SatDSiG and similar remote-sensing regimes; 'Earth Observation' is asked but never the data characteristics
- Cyber posture in the standalone EU Space Act wizard — zero security questions despite Art. 74-95 producing a 'Cybersecurity' module verdict in the result
- Insurance status in the standalone EU Space Act wizard — the Insurance & Liability module (Art. 44-51) is assigned without asking whether any coverage exists
- Debris-plan maturity — a single yes/no in unified only; no questions on passivation, casualty risk assessment, trackability, or post-mission disposal reliability anywhere
- Human spaceflight / suborbital activity — relevant to UK SIA scoping (the UK profile hardcodes involvesPeople:false, isSuborbital:false) and to the 'Space Tourism' service type that is otherwise ignored
- In-orbit servicing specifics (RPO target, cooperative vs non-cooperative, licensing of the serviced object) — ISOS is one click with no follow-up
- Multi-country establishment — only one establishment country can be selected, though real operators have multiple establishments that change both NIS2 main-establishment jurisdiction and national-law overlap
- Operator's role in the value chain for the EU Space Act (owner vs operator vs procuring entity) — 'operator' is assumed; ownership/control separation, common in leased-capacity arrangements, is never probed

## 3. Ziel-Architektur

ONE PROFILE SPINE, ONE BRANCHING QUESTION GRAPH, EXISTING ENGINES AS PLUGGABLE VERDICT MODULES. (1) Operator Profile spine: a single canonical, versioned profile object (new Prisma model, not a JSON blob on User) that every tier reads and writes — quick-check answers carry forward into the full assessment (never ask twice, GOV.UK question protocol), and the living tier diffs against it. (2) Question graph as data: questions, options, branch conditions ('showIf'), tier tags, and 'why we ask this' citations live in one declarative dataset; branch conditions are evaluated SERVER-side at calculate time as well as client-side for display, so no gate can be bypassed by direct API calls. Every answer is a tri-state-capable value: chosen option | explicitly-unsure | not-asked (branch skipped) — three distinct stored states, never coerced. (3) Verdict pipeline (one calculate route per tier): [a] applicability gates server-enforced (defense-only Art 2(3), pre-application-date assets, no EU nexus) → honest cited out-of-scope verdicts; [b] NIS2 gateway classification FIRST — per the Council Art 75a two-tier architecture, NIS2 status determines which cyber regime applies, so classifyNIS2Entity runs before the Space Act engine and gains a fourth output state 'needs-clarification' (insufficient data is never 'out of scope'); [c] EU Space Act engine invoked per role (reusing the existing per-activity-type invocation + merger), now actually consuming constellation tier, propulsion, mass class, and CDR timing; [d] national space-law engines for held + considered jurisdictions (UK delegation failure becomes a visible fidelity flag, not a silent fallback); [e] adjacent-regime modules: spectrum engine (finally wired into the assessment), export-control flags, UN-registration findings, national insurance minimums; [f] merger (reused) → obligation map. (4) Rulebook versioning (OpenFisca pattern): every engine dataset carries a semantic version + per-source as-of dates (COM(2025) 335 Commission text, Council general approach 5 Dec 2025 / Cyprus compromise 30 Mar 2026, ITRE draft 3 Mar 2026, each MS transposition); every stored result is pinned to the rulebook version it was computed against; obligations where the three legislative texts diverge carry a machine-readable 'contested' flux flag with the per-text positions. A rulebook bump is a change-trigger for the living tier. (5) Explanation envelope (reuse the Passage pattern): every finding ships {verdict, legal basis citation, instrument + as-of date, confidence state, why-this-applies trace back to the answers that triggered it}. (6) Single article dataset: the tracker/dashboard import is regenerated from caelex-eu-space-act-engine.json (or imports the stored engine result directly); src/data/articles.ts as an independent applicability source is retired.

## 4. Fragenkatalog (72 Fragen, mit Branching + Warum)

> QUICK = Quick-Check-Tier; FULL = nur Voll-Assessment. Kritiker-Korrekturen in §7 einarbeiten (insb. 4-5 Streichungen + 2 Gate-Fixes + fehlende Cluster).

- === SECTION 1: IDENTITY & ROLE (profile-first, ENISA pattern — these answers scope everything downstream) ===
- Q1.1 [QUICK] 'Which roles describe your organisation?' (multi-select) — Spacecraft operator / Launch operator / Launch-site operator / In-space service provider (servicing, refuelling, debris removal, inspection) / Collision-avoidance or SSA service provider / Primary provider of space-based data / Ground-segment operator (ground stations, TT&C, mission control) / Hosted-payload owner / Reseller-distributor of space data / Component or subsystem supplier. WHY: the master branching dimension — each role attaches a distinct EU Space Act obligation cluster (the 119-article engine's operator-type filters: SCO/LO/LSO/ISOS/CAP/PDP), ground-segment operation triggers NIS2 Annex I 'Space', hosted-payload owners can be in scope without controlling the host spacecraft (Cooley reading of Art 17 indirect delivery), component suppliers are largely outside the Space Act but inside export control. WHEN: always, first question.
- Q1.2 [QUICK] 'Where is your organisation established?' — primary country (EU27 / EEA / UK / CH / US / other), [FULL] plus additional establishments (multi). WHY: the applicability gate — EU establishment → Union operator needing MS authorisation (Art 6; Council text: MS of main establishment); third country → Art 17 registration path; multiple establishments shift NIS2 main-establishment jurisdiction and multiply national-law overlap (a dimension the current product cannot capture at all). WHEN: always.
- Q1.3 [FULL] 'Which is your MAIN place of establishment?' WHY: the Council text assigns the authorising authority — and NIS2 jurisdiction — to the main establishment. WHEN: only if >1 establishment given.
- Q1.4 [QUICK] 'Are you a research or educational institution?' Y/N. WHY: Art 10 light-regime eligibility + Art 62 technical carve-outs; also Italian Law 89/2025 insurance reductions. WHEN: always.
- Q1.5 [QUICK] 'Headcount band?' (1-9 / 10-49 / 50-249 / 250+) and 'Annual turnover band?' (<€2M / €2-10M / €10-50M / >€50M). WHY: double duty — EU SME definition (Space Act light regime, EP draft's 'small mid-cap' extension) AND the NIS2 size-cap (≥250 or >€50M → essential; ≥50 or ≥€10M → important). Bands, not exact figures, to cut friction and don't-know rates (Lane C). WHEN: always.
- Q1.6 [FULL] 'Balance-sheet total band?' (≤€10M / ≤€43M / >€43M / unsure). WHY: both the SME definition and NIS2 caps use turnover OR balance sheet — never asked today; 'unsure' takes the conservative (larger) reading. WHEN: full tier, always.

- Q1.9 [QUICK] 'Are your space assets used EXCLUSIVELY for defence or national security?' Y/N. WHY: Art 2(3) exclusion — a genuine hard stop, but now enforced SERVER-side (today it is client-only and bypassable by direct API call); partial/dual-use stays in scope and is noted. WHEN: always.

WHEN: role includes spacecraft operator.

WHY: Art 66 manoeuvrability, Art 64 collision-avoidance feasibility, Art 73(1) constellation propulsion mandate; 'none' + constellation ≥10 produces a named red-flag finding. WHEN: spacecraft operator.
- Q2.4 [FULL] 'Largest spacecraft wet-mass class?' — <10kg / 10-100kg / 100-500kg / >500kg / unsure. WHY: debris-mitigation class, re-entry casualty risk, and national TPL insurance minimums all scale with mass; the current unified helpText falsely claims mass is used — this makes the claim true. WHEN: spacecraft operator.
- Q2.5 [FULL] 'Trackability means fitted or planned?' — GNSS beacon / laser retroreflector / SSA-provider registration / none / unsure. WHY: Art 63 trackability obligation (binds Union operators; ESPI flags the third-country asymmetry — finding text adapts to establishment). WHEN: spacecraft operator.
- Q2.6 [FULL] 'Launch vehicle class and expected launches per year?' WHY: launch-provider authorisation fees scale by vehicle capacity (€200k-1.5M, impact-assessment estimate) and launcher re-entry safeguards (Arts 58-61) attach. WHEN: role includes launch operator.

## 5. Scoring-Modell

NOT A SCORE — A PIPELINE FROM FACTS TO AN OBLIGATION MAP, WITH MONOTONIC ROUND-UP. Stage 1, APPLICABILITY GATES (server-enforced, every field required with explicit-unknown allowed — an empty payload is a validation error, never a verdict): defense-only (Art 2(3)), all-assets-pre-application-date (with the contested-date scenario note), no-EU-nexus → honest cited out-of-scope results reusing buildOutOfScopeResult. Stage 2, NIS2 GATEWAY FIRST (Council Art 75a two-tier architecture): extended classifyNIS2Entity consumes ground-segment fact + ECN exclusion + size-cap with balance-sheet and group aggregation + designation + Art 2(2)(b)-(e) exceptions + the now-reachable Art 26 path; outputs essential / important / out-of-scope / NEEDS-CLARIFICATION (insufficient data or unsure on a decisive input is NEVER rendered as 'does not apply' — fixes the current false-green). NIS2 status then routes the cyber cluster: NIS2-classified → NIS2 Art 21 obligations under the applicable national transposition; NIS2-out-of-scope but Space-Act-in-scope → Space Act resilience articles directly; both shown when the legislative texts diverge, with the flux flag. Stage 3, REGIME DETERMINATION as a real eligibility check, not a one-liner: light regime = research status OR (SME per EU definition INCLUDING balance-sheet alternative and linked-enterprise aggregation), output as eligible / likely eligible (verify: group figures) / not eligible — with the reasoning chain shown. Stage 4, OBLIGATION MAP: per article/requirement across all engines — applicable / conditionally applicable (condition named) / not applicable / contested-in-legislation (Commission vs Council vs EP positions stated); each with citation, instrument, as-of date (Passage explanation envelope). Stage 5, READINESS DIFF (full tier only): per applicable obligation, the 4-state evidence answer (evidenced / undocumented / partial / missing) plus 'unsure'→gap; aggregated per cluster as 'N of M obligations evidenced' bands — no single overall number by default; optional CyFun-style spider for management reporting, labeled as maturity-not-compliance. UNCERTAINTY RULES (invariants, unit-tested): (1) 'unsure' is a stored first-class state, never coerced (PMC don't-know research: prevalence jumps to 47-63% when offered — that signal is the product, surfaced as a named 'Unknowns to resolve' section); (2) monotonic round-up — adding an 'unsure' can only widen the obligation set or lower confidence, never remove an obligation or improve readiness; (3) no fabricated findings — if a cross-reference lookup finds nothing, the report says 'none identified', period (kills the hardcoded 'structural overlaps'); (4) every estimate keeps its provenance label through redaction (the 'Internal estimate — not empirically validated' tag survives to the user; fee figures labeled 'Commission impact-assessment estimate, not an enacted fee schedule'). CONFIDENCE BANDS per finding, derived purely from answer epistemic states (never from tone): DETERMINED (all inputs in the trigger chain answered), PROBABLE (one unknown in the chain — what to clarify is named), INDETERMINATE (decisive input unknown — 'answer X or consult counsel'). Contradiction detection (e.g. third-country-serving-EU + servesEU=No) blocks at the check-your-answers step rather than silently accepting.

### 7.1 Web-verifizierte Rechtsfehler im Blueprint (MUST-FIX)

- 'Council general approach 5 Dec 2025' is wrong twice over (web-verified): the 5 Dec 2025 text was a Danish PRESIDENCY compromise, not a general approach, and as of June 2026 the Council has adopted NO position at all — the 29 May 2026 COMPET took only a progress report, flagged unresolved issues (military/dual-use autonomy, third-country operators) and passed the file to the Irish presidency. - The load-bearing 'Council Art 75a two-tier architecture' citation is unverified and likely invented. Gleiss Lutz describes the Council's 5 Dec 2025 approach as SYNCHRONISATION — Space Act 'without prejudice to NIS2', with Art 75 et seq. applying only to operators below the NIS2 Art 3 thresholds plus third-country operators — no Art 75a anywhere. The NIS2-gateway-first pipeline is still defensible in substance, but the EP/ITRE draft (3 Mar 2026, date verified) deletes the resilience chapter entirely and extends NIS2 via a new Art 117a — so 'NIS2-out-of-scope → Space Act resilience articles' is itself a contested-text position that needs the same flux flag the blueprint applies elsewhere, not the architecture's silent default.

### Unsicherheiten / im Fluss

- The final cybersecurity architecture of the EU Space Act is fully in flux: Commission lex specialis (Art 75) vs Council two-tier NIS2-first (Art 75a, Cyprus compromise 30 Mar 2026) vs Parliament deletion of the resilience chapter (NIS2 integration via Art 117a). Which model prevails determines whether an operator's cyber obligations flow from NIS2 implementing acts or directly from the Space Act — an assessment should branch on both until trilogue concludes.
- Application date and transition periods are contested: 1 Jan 2030 fixed date (Commission) vs 36 months after entry into force (Council and Parliament), with transition windows ranging from ~2 years to 8 years and different CDR thresholds (12 vs 24 months). No trilogue had started or been scheduled per sources available as of May 2026; adoption could slip such that application lands well after 2030.
- Article numbers cited from the Commission text (e.g. Arts 64, 73(1), 75, 96-98, 105, 118-119) have already shifted in compromise texts (new 75a, 117a, 118a) and may be renumbered again in the final act.
- Third-country operator regime details (registration thresholds, enforcement against operators with no EU establishment, equivalence decisions — none adopted) are unresolved; ESPI flags enforcement as an open question; the Council/Parliament also removed international organisations from direct scope, contingent on bilateral agreements.
- Penalty design is unsettled: Commission caps fines at 2% worldwide turnover/2x profits, Parliament removes caps and assessment criteria in favour of Commission discretion via delegated acts.
- Fee figures (EUR 100k/platform, EUR 200k-1.5M launch fees, EUR 68M constellation-authorisation savings) come from the Commission impact assessment as estimates reported by law firms — they are not enacted fee schedules.
- Whether insurance/liability harmonisation gets added during the legislative process is unknown; as of the texts reviewed it remains national law (industry is lobbying for harmonised liability caps).
- Germany's Weltraumgesetz timing is uncertain (key-points paper Sept 2024, stalled by government change, drafting expected to track the EU Space Act); Spain's draft space law timing similarly unverified. The exact in-force date of the German NIS2UmsuCG (project memory: 6 Dec 2025) was corroborated only as 'enacted in 2025' by the sources fetched, not to the day.
- NIS2 transposition completeness as of June 2026 could not be verified state-by-state; the firmest data points are the 7 May 2025 reasoned opinions to 19 MS and reports that DE/PT/AT have since adopted laws with ES/FR/PL close — no CJEU referrals were found, but a referral decision after the sources' publication cannot be ruled out.
- The exact NIS2 Annex I 'Space' sector wording (ground-based infrastructure supporting space-based services, excluding public ECN providers) was confirmed via secondary sources and the Commission digital-strategy page rather than a direct EUR-Lex fetch in this session.
- No official Commission applicability checker or Q&A for the EU Space Act exists yet (only the DG DEFIS page, factsheet, impact assessment, and the closed July-Nov 2025 consultation); if the Commission publishes implementing-act-level guidance or a Q&A later, the assessment dimensions should be re-validated against it.
- Hogan Lovells and White & Case analyses returned HTTP 403; their positions (e.g. 'material divergences' framing, Dec 2025 Council text reading) were captured via search-result snippets only and could not be verified against the full articles.
- Counts of national space laws vary by source (EPRS: 'at least 13 laws in force by end-2024' including Cyprus 2023; Loyens & Loeff lists 13 MS excluding Cyprus but including Greece) — the precise list depends on what counts as a 'space law' (full authorisation regime vs registry-only acts).
